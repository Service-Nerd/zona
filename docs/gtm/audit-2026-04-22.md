# GTM Audit — Zona
**Date:** 2026-04-22
**Prepared by:** CMO perspective — commercial audit, not brand agency work

---

## 1. Executive Summary

Three things matter most. First: the differentiator is real but undersold. Zone discipline enforcement is not a feature — it is the product. Everything Zona does flows from "you are training too hard on your easy days." That is a credible, observable problem with a growing cultural tailwind (Zone 2 has NPR coverage, longevity podcasts, Peter Attia — it is a mainstream concept in 2026). Zona is positioned exactly at the right moment. Second: the trial-to-paid conversion moment is the product's weakest commercial surface. Every new user gets the full paid experience for 14 days — the zone discipline score, weekly report, and Strava coaching are all visible during the trial. The risk is not that free users never see the differentiator; it is that the upgrade prompt when the trial ends does not remind them specifically what they are about to lose. Third: there is no front door. No marketing site, no App Store presence. The product cannot be found. Every other decision is downstream of this one.

**The one decision that would move the business forward fastest this week:** write and publish a marketing landing page. Everything else is optimisation of a product nobody can discover.

## 2. Differentiator Audit

### Category map

| App | Core positioning | Price (UK, monthly equiv.) | Primary user |
|---|---|---|---|
| Runna | Personalised running plans, professional coaching feel | £8.33/mo (£99.99/yr) | Ambitious recreational runners |
| TrainingPeaks | Data-driven training management, coach/athlete workflow | £11.25/mo ($134.99/yr) | Serious amateur, semi-pro, coached athletes |
| Garmin Coach | Free adaptive plans, device-native | Free (device required) | Garmin hardware owners |
| Nike Run Club | Free guided runs, non-adaptive plans | Free | Casual/social runners |
| Strava | Segment competition, social layer, route planning | £4.58/mo (£54.99/yr) | Activity loggers, social runners |
| Coopah | Coach-chat, community, beginner-friendly | £7/mo | Beginners, first-time marathon runners |
| McMillan | Science-backed plans, coach access | $39.99/mo | Performance-focused runners |
| **Zona** | Zone discipline enforcement, honest coaching against overtraining | £5/mo (£59.99/yr) | Non-elite runners who overtrain |

**Where Zona sits:** Zona occupies a gap between free/social apps (Nike, Garmin, Strava) and premium plan apps (Runna, TrainingPeaks). The price point is deliberately below Runna and TrainingPeaks. The positioning is differentiated: none of the competitors lead with "you are training too hard." Runna's stance is positive and supportive ("personalised plans that work"). TrainingPeaks is data-management for the serious athlete. Garmin Coach is adaptive but device-locked and free. Zona is the only app with an antagonistic positioning toward the user's instinct to overdo it.

**Source for competitor prices:** Runna pricing page; TrainingPeaks DC Rainmaker, April 2025; Coopah TechRadar review; Strava MoneySavingExpert April 2026.

### "Why now" test

Zone 2 training crossed from specialist endurance coaching knowledge into mass culture in approximately 2024–2025. NPR ran a primer in October 2025. Andrew Huberman, Peter Attia, and longevity researchers made it mainstream. The runner who reads about mitochondrial efficiency at lunch and then goes out at 7pm and runs hard anyway is the Zona user. The gap between knowing Zone 2 matters and actually doing it is the product opportunity. In 2020 that gap did not exist in mainstream awareness. In 2026 it does.

**This is a genuine "why now." The timing is defensible.**

### Defensibility test

**If Runna launched "honest coaching mode" tomorrow, what would Zona still own?**

Very little in terms of features — Runna already has plan adaptation, HR integration, and coaching feedback. What Zona would retain is the brand *position*. "Slow down. You're not Kipchoge." is a posture, not a feature. Zona is built on the premise that the user is the problem. Runna is built on the premise that the user is a hero who just needs the right tools. These are different products for different psychological profiles. Runna's user wants to be enabled. Zona's user wants to be told the truth, even when it is uncomfortable.

**The defensibility is in tone and in the trust that accrues from honest feedback — not in a technical moat.**

The technical infrastructure (zone discipline score, hybrid rule + AI engine, Strava-derived aerobic pace) is a necessary condition but not the differentiator. Runna could rebuild it in three months with sufficient engineering. What they cannot rebuild quickly is the brand permission to say "you ran too hot. That's not an easy run — that's a moderate run wearing easy-run clothes."

### Wedge statement

*Zona is for the runner who already knows they should slow down and does not.*

One user: a 38-year-old with a structured job, 4 runs a week, a half-marathon target, and a consistent pattern of running their easy days at tempo pace because it "feels more productive." They have Strava. They know their zone. They ignore it. Zona is the only product built specifically for this person's failure mode.

### Believability test

**Does the codebase actually deliver on "honest coaching" or is it marketing the engineering has not yet earned?**

Mostly earned, with one gap.

What is earned:
- `getZonaReflectResponse()` (DashboardClient.tsx, line 1145) produces session-type-aware, RPE-calibrated responses. "A touch warm for an easy day. Worth noting." is specific and honest.
- The zone discipline score (weekly report, CoachScreen) quantifies compliance against Zone 2 ceiling in a metric a user can act on.
- `buildSessionFeedbackPrompt()` (sessionFeedback.ts) uses few-shot examples that are genuinely Zona in voice — "that's not an easy run — that's a moderate run wearing easy-run clothes" is specific and will not be said by any competitor's AI.
- `getRestCopy()` (DashboardClient.tsx, line 2754) produces phase-aware, fitness-level-aware rest day copy that acknowledges what the user is likely thinking.
- The AdjustmentBanner and plan adjustment triggers (load_spike, zone_drift, shadow_load) are real coaching signals, not gamification.

What is not yet earned:
- The zone discipline score (the primary coaching metric per the brief) is visible on the Coach screen but is not explained anywhere a new user will read it. There is no in-product definition of what score is good, bad, or what to do with it. A score of 72 means nothing to a runner who has never seen it before.
- Strava is required for the best coaching signals (avg HR, zone % compliance). Free users who have not connected Strava — which includes all free users, since Strava is PAID — receive rule-engine plans and RPE-only feedback. For those users the "honest coaching" promise is partially unfulfilled.
- The confidence score (generated plan preview) has short explanatory text but no coaching context: "Your fitness and timeline line up" does not tell the user what to do differently if the score is 5/10 rather than 8/10.

**Differentiator verdict: Real. The foundation exists. The coaching infrastructure is more sophisticated than any competitor in the sub-£10/month bracket. The commercial risk is not that the product is fake — it is that users will not understand what makes it different until they are 3 weeks in.**

### Top 3 confusion risks

1. **"Another training app."** Without a marketing site or App Store presence, a user who hears about Zona has no way to understand the positioning before they sign up. First contact is the login screen. The login screen shows "Do less. Improve more." — four words that do not explain the overtraining thesis or the zone discipline model.

2. **Free users may not understand they are using a different product.** A free user gets a rule-engine plan (no AI, no Strava, no coaching). A paid user gets AI enrichment, zone discipline scoring, aerobic pace derivation, weekly coaching reports. These are meaningfully different products, but the app calls both "your plan." Free users who compare Zona to Runna at the free tier and find Runna's free week superior will churn.

3. **"Zone 2" as a concept is still opaque.** The app assumes zone literacy — it shows HR ceilings, zones, and discipline scores without explaining the model to new users. The orientation screen (OrientationScreen, DashboardClient.tsx line 837) gives a Zone 2 ceiling and says "walk until it drops," which is the right behaviour — but a runner who has never heard of Zone 2 training will not understand why.

## 3. Tiering Audit

### Free tier assessment: confused identity

**What free users get (per feature-registry.md and confirmed in code):**
- Rule-based plan generation for 5K / 10K / Half Marathon
- Session display and tracking
- Formula-derived pace and HR targets
- Manual session log
- Session history
- Basic profile
- Theme toggle

**What free users do not get:**
- AI-enriched plans (no coach_intro, no confidence_score, no session labels)
- Strava integration (no aerobic pace derivation, no avg HR from real runs)
- Weekly coaching report (no zone discipline score in context)
- Plan adjustments
- AI session feedback (RunFeedbackCard)
- Marathon / 50K / 100K distances

**Is the free tier generous enough to earn trust?**

This question is largely answered by the reverse trial model. Every new user gets full paid access for 14 days — zone discipline score, weekly coaching report, Strava integration, AI session feedback — before any paywall appears. The free tier's identity does not need to carry the sales argument, because the trial does that job.

The correct reframe: the free tier post-trial does not need more features or sharper framing. It needs a better transition moment at day 14. The UpgradeScreen headline is "Your trial's done. Time to make it official." — this is adequate but generic. It should name what the user is losing specifically: "Your zone discipline coaching pauses here." or "No more weekly reports until you subscribe." The user has experienced the product. The upgrade prompt should reference that experience, not describe the features abstractly.

**The diagnosis:** The free tier is not the problem. The trial-exit moment is. A user who experienced 14 days of zone discipline coaching and does not upgrade either did not find it valuable (a product problem) or was not reminded clearly enough what they would lose (a copy problem). The copy problem is fixable in an afternoon.

### Paid tier assessment: feature scatter

**Current paid feature list (UpgradeScreen.tsx):**
- AI training plans — "Built around your race, not a template."
- Strava sync — "Your actual paces. Not guesses."
- AI coaching — "Session feedback that knows your plan."
- Dynamic reshaping — "Miss a week. The plan adapts."

**Can a user say in one sentence what they get by upgrading?**

Not comfortably. The four features are accurate but do not cohere into a single upgrade promise. They are individually correct but collectively they read as "more stuff." Compare to: *"Zona paid turns your Strava data into zone discipline coaching — it tells you why your plan isn't working, and adjusts it."* That is what the paid tier actually is.

**Recurring value scores per paid feature:**

| Feature | Recurring value | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| AI plan generation | Medium | High at onboarding; low ongoing. A user generates one plan and runs it for 12–16 weeks. No recurring value until race cycle repeats. |
| Strava sync | High | Every run generates new data, new coaching signal. Value compounds as training history builds. |
| Weekly coaching report | High | Weekly cadence, directly addresses the overtraining thesis, hard to replicate elsewhere. |
| Zone discipline score | High | The primary coaching metric. Recalculated every week. Directly actionable. |
| Dynamic plan adjustments | High | Recurring value every time the user deviates — which is frequently, given the target user profile. |
| Confidence score (plan preview) | Low | One-time. Interesting at plan generation, unused thereafter. |
| AI session feedback (RunFeedbackCard) | High | Per-session, tied to real Strava data. The most immediate paid-tier payoff. |

**The highest recurring value features are also the least prominent in the upgrade prompt.** The UpgradeScreen lists four items in order: AI plans, Strava sync, AI coaching, Dynamic reshaping. The weekly report and zone discipline score — which are the ongoing proof of value — are not mentioned. This is a commercial miss.

### Price ladder coherence

Zona: £7.99/month, £59.99/year (£5/month equivalent, 37% discount).

Category comparison:
- Runna: £15.99/month, £99.99/year (£8.33/month, 48% discount)
- TrainingPeaks: ~£15.87/month, ~£106/year (£8.83/month, 44% discount)
- Coopah: £7/month (no annual pricing found)
- Strava: £8.99/month, £54.99/year (£4.58/month, 49% discount)

**Zona's annual discount is 37%, which is low for the category.** Category norm is 44–49%. At £7.99/month and £59.99/year, the annual proposition is "save £36 over the year" — which is not very compelling because the monthly price is already low. The more commercially aggressive move would be to raise the monthly price to £9.99 and keep the annual at £59.99 (£5/month, 50% discount). This creates a stronger annual incentive and frames the monthly price as premium rather than cheap.

**Assumption A1:** Current pricing was set conservatively to undercut Runna. Validate whether price sensitivity research supports this — or whether it is a default assumption.

### Gating errors

| Issue | Severity | Detail |
|---|---|---|
| Zone discipline score is gated | Medium | The score is only shown on the Coach screen (paid). Free users who complete sessions and log RPE generate underlying data but never see a zone compliance signal. If the zone discipline is the core product thesis, some form of it should be free. | 
| Strava is PAID | Medium | This gates the primary data source for honest coaching. Free users logging manually cannot get aerobic pace derivation or HR compliance analysis. A partially connected free tier (Strava read-only, limited analysis) would accelerate free-to-paid conversion by showing users what they are missing. |
| Plan confidence score is visible on preview | Low | Free users generating plans do not see the confidence score, but they did see the ConfidenceBadge component is conditional on `meta.confidence_score`. This is correct — but the teaser card (TeaserCard, GeneratePlanScreen.tsx line 305) says "Want a more personal plan?" without naming what is missing. It should name the confidence score. |

### Upgrade trigger moments, ranked by conversion likelihood

1. **First rest-day-after-a-breach (High):** User runs easy day at RPE 7, Zona says "A touch warm for an easy day. Worth noting." — this is the moment a user most wants to know "how am I actually doing?" The Coach tab and zone discipline score gate sits exactly here. CTA: "See your zone discipline score in Coach."

2. **Plan generation — Step 3 teaser (Medium-High):** Free users see the teaser card before generating. Conversion likelihood depends on copy quality. Current copy ("Want a more personal plan?") is generic. Naming what is missing ("Marathon distances, terrain, injury history, and a confidence score for your goal") would lift conversion.

3. **First week with Strava data (Medium):** A user who connects Strava as a free user will see aerobic pace in session cards (if the feature is correctly gated) — but not HR analysis or the weekly report. The delta between what they see and what they cannot see is the conversion moment.

4. **Trial expiry on first return visit (Medium):** The UpgradeScreen headline is "Your trial's done. Time to make it official." This is fine. The CTA is "Get Zona Premium." The upgrade promise on this screen should lead with the weekly report / zone discipline score, not AI plans — because the user has already experienced 14 days and knows what they got.

5. **Marathon distance gate (Low-Medium):** Attempting to select Marathon, 50K, or 100K and hitting the lock prompt. Low volume — most free users will not be training for distances longer than a half marathon. High intent from users who do.

**One-sentence upgrade promise (recommended):** *"Zona paid turns your Strava data into weekly zone discipline coaching — and adjusts your plan when it matters."*

1. **"Get Zona Premium" → "Start your subscription"** — UpgradeScreen.tsx, line 207. Every word of the paywall matters. "Premium" is the most overused word in fitness apps and contradicts the honest, anti-gloss positioning.

6. **Coach screen empty state: "Log some sessions and runs to generate this week's coaching report." → "Log a few sessions first. The report needs something to work with."** — DashboardClient.tsx, line 3681. Small change. Removes the instructional tone, adds Zona voice.

Either promote it to an official brand asset or replace with "Do less. Improve more." for consistency.

## 5. Visual Consistency Audit

### Visual system verdict

**Coherent. The palette is locked and enforced. Typography is consistent. Session type colour coding is applied correctly. There are no palette regressions visible in the source files reviewed.**

The System B palette (navy dark `#0B132B`, teal `#5BC0BE`, amber `#F2C14E`, off-white `#F7F9FB`) is referenced via CSS custom properties throughout. No hardcoded hex values were observed in any of the reviewed component files. The two-font system (Inter as `var(--font-ui)`, Space Grotesk as `var(--font-brand)`) is applied consistently and the `--font-display` bug (noted in CLAUDE.md) was not observed in any reviewed file.

### Inconsistencies found

| Issue | File | Line(s) | Severity |
|---|---|---|---|
| "effort-first training" sub-tagline appears only on OrientationScreen | DashboardClient.tsx | ~886 | Low — brand inconsistency, not visual inconsistency |

This is borderline — it is a symbol, not an emoji. Consider replacing with a Unicode check character or SVG icon for absolute compliance. |

### Brand asset hygiene

**Cannot fully audit.** The following items require direct inspection that is outside the scope of a code audit:
- `public/icon-192.png` referenced in sw.js — file existence confirmed; quality/design not verified
- `public/google-logo.svg` referenced in login — functional, design origin unverified
- Apple touch icon, OG image, manifest.json — not reviewed in scope

**Flagged gap:** No `/public/og-image.jpg` or equivalent was observed in the file tree review. For a product without a marketing site, the OG image on the `zona.app` URL is the only visual impression a user gets when a link is shared. This should be treated as a marketing asset.

### What to preserve through a rebrand

If a name or style change is under consideration, the following should survive:

1. **The left-accent-bar session card pattern.** This is the primary UI metaphor and is visually distinctive. It appears on session cards, feature lists in the upgrade screen, teaser cards, and coach notes. It is the Zona visual unit.
2. **The teal (#5BC0BE) as the action/success/positive signal colour.** This is differentiated from the standard blue-or-green choices most fitness apps make.
3. **The amber coaching signal.** Using amber (not red) for warnings is a deliberate brand decision that communicates honest concern rather than alarm.
4. **The two-font system hierarchy.** Space Grotesk for headings/brand creates a mechanical-athletic feel that is unusual in the category (most competitors use rounded sans-serifs that feel "wellness"). The contrast with Inter (very clean utility) gives Zona a distinct typographic character.
5. **The compact metric strip pattern on session cards** (zone · HR · pace · distance). This hierarchy is correct and mirrors the information runners actually want at a glance.

### Priority fixes before any rebrand

1. Standardise the tagline — "Do less. Improve more." appears in login and loading screens; "effort-first training" appears in OrientationScreen; "Slow down. You're not Kipchoge." appears at the footer of the login page. These are three different first impressions. Pick one.
2. Audit the OG/social image — if there is none, any shared URL looks blank.
3. Confirm that `var(--font-brand)` references are not accidentally using `--font-display` in any file not reviewed here.

## 8. What Not To Do

Five initiatives that look commercially sensible but would be wrong for Zona right now.

**1. Do not build a group / community feature.**
Several competitors (Coopah, Strava) lead with community. For Zona's user, community is anti-brand. The Zona user is the person who overtrained because they saw someone else running fast on Strava. Adding group challenges, leaderboards, or a social feed would directly undermine the "do less" thesis. It would also attract a different user: the performance-motivated social runner who already has Strava. This user does not need an honest coach — they need an audience. That is a different product.

**2. Do not launch a "beginner" tier or a "beginner" plan.**
Coopah is explicitly positioned for beginners. Garmin Coach and Nike Run Club are accessible to beginners. Zona's brand authority derives from honest coaching — which requires the user to have a problem. Beginners do not yet have the overtraining problem; they may not be running consistently enough to develop zone discipline failure modes. A "beginner" tier dilutes the brand and puts Zona in direct feature competition with free apps.

**3. Do not build a rich dashboard.**
The brand principle "No dashboards or noise" (ux-principles.md) is commercially correct as well as aesthetically correct. A dashboard with charts, trend lines, weekly/monthly summaries, and historical comparisons is what TrainingPeaks sells at £11.25/month. Zona at £5/month that competes on data richness will always lose. The Zona value is signal extraction — "here is the one thing that matters this week." Adding charts and graphs would cost engineering time, confuse the positioning, and attract the data-obsessed user who already subscribes to TrainingPeaks.

**4. Do not build a Kipchoge / elite runner content layer.**
The tagline references Kipchoge. This will prompt someone to suggest elite runner content, aspirational training contexts, or "what Kipchoge does differently" feature angles. These would be directionally wrong. The brand premise is "you are not Kipchoge." Zona wins by acknowledging the gap and building tools for the actual runner, not by aspiring to close it. Any content that glorifies elite performance undermines the core truth.

**5. Do not run a black Friday / promotional discount campaign before a marketing site exists.**
A discount campaign would bring users to a product with no front door. They would arrive at the login screen, see "Do less. Improve more." with no context, and convert at low rates. Worse: a discount on a £7.99/month product (making it ~£5.99) against a product already priced low trains users to wait for discounts and devalues the subscription. Fix the front door first.

## Gaps Flagged (Cannot Audit)

The following are explicitly flagged as gaps that this audit cannot fully evaluate, per the brief.

**No marketing site.** `app/page.tsx` redirects to `/dashboard`. There is no user-facing page at zona.app that explains the product to someone who is not already signed in. This is the most significant go-to-market gap. Any word-of-mouth, any shared link, any App Store click-through lands at a login screen. First impression is four words and an email input.

**No App Store listing.** Neither an iOS App Store Connect listing nor a Google Play Store listing is present in the codebase. Without a native app listing, Zona cannot benefit from organic app store discovery, ratings, reviews, or the social proof that drives conversion for this category. Runna's App Store presence (with structured plans imagery, social proof, and editorial featuring) is a significant competitive advantage that cannot be addressed without building this.

**No email templates beyond Supabase auth defaults.** The trial model (14 days full access, graceful downgrade) has a critical touchpoint: the moment a user's trial ends. Supabase auth sends a confirmation email on sign-up. There is no trial expiry email, no "you have 3 days of trial remaining" nudge, no re-engagement email for users who generated a plan but have not logged a session. Email automation at the trial boundary is a standard conversion lever that is entirely absent.
